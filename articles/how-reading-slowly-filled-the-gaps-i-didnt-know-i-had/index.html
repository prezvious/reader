At the start of this year, I made a simple resolution: 12 books in 12 months. One book per month. So far, so good — nothing too ambitious.

January: Moth Smoke by Mohsin Hamid.
February: Poonachi by Perumal Murugan.
March: Wise and Otherwise by Sudha Murty.
Now, in April, I am reading The Vegetarian by Han Kang.

I keep my books intentionally short, usually under 200 pages. Knowing my attention and routine, I guess if a book feels too long, I might stop midway. Right now, the goal is just to be consistent.

Finishing a small book every month feels much better than leaving a big book unfinished for a year, right? There's a real sense of accomplishment in finishing a book, and then ordering a new one and unboxing the parcel feels like a small reward I give myself. It's addictive.

But this is not about what I am reading. It is about what reading is doing to me.

The Quiet Shift

Reading has quietly become the thing I look forward to at the end of the day. Earlier, when the day felt taxing, I would scroll on my phone, thinking it would relax me. But somehow, it only made my mind more crowded.

Now, I read a few pages before sleeping, and it feels like I am gently closing the door on the day instead of carrying it to bed with me.

Books have become my way of distracting myself from the noise — not in a careless way, but in a healing way.

When I read, my mind goes somewhere else for a while. The problems are still there, the tasks are still there, but they stop shouting. They wait. And in that small space, I get to breathe.

Filling the Gaps

The best part is this: reading is filling gaps in my life that I didn't even know existed. The gap before sleep. The gap when I don't want to think about the day anymore. The gap where I would scroll mindlessly and get restless.

Reading sits in that space quietly and does something my phone never could: it slows my mind down and opens my imagination at the same time.

Reading is the only thing I've found that quiets my mind and feeds it at the same time.

Staying Close to Words

And since I am writing less these days, reading also makes me feel like I am still in touch with words. Like I haven't completely left writing behind. It feels like a small thread connecting me to the part of me that loves to write, even when life gets busy.

I don't want to lose that spark. So even if I am not writing every day, at least I am reading. At least I am still close to words.

I started reading to finish books, but somewhere along the way, reading started finishing my days better. So yeah, reading is highly recommended. It does make a difference in your life and personality.
