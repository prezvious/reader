If you study the decisions that shape a life, you'll notice something strange: the bad ones rarely look bad in the moment. They look reasonable. Defensible. Even smart. The thing that ruins them isn't the choice itself — it's the half-commitment that follows. Most decisions don't fail because they were wrong. They fail because they were never fully made.

The Quiet Cost of Hedging

When you decide something while leaving the door cracked open behind you, you pay a tax you can't see. Part of your attention stays on the alternative you didn't pick. Part of your energy goes into preserving the option to retreat. And the people around you — colleagues, partners, collaborators — feel the wobble even when you don't say a word. They start hedging too.

A decision that's only 70% committed is, in practice, almost identical to no decision at all. The remaining 30% is where the leakage happens.

The opposite of a decision isn't indecision. It's a decision you keep secretly renegotiating with yourself.

What "Sticking" Actually Looks Like

A decision sticks when three things are true at once. First, you've stopped litigating it inside your own head. Second, you've changed something visible — a calendar, a file, a conversation — so that backing out has a cost. Third, you've told someone else, in plain language, what you're going to do.

None of these is dramatic. None of them requires courage in the heroic sense. They just require that you cross a threshold from thinking about the decision to acting as if it's already true.

The two-list test

Here's a small exercise. Take a decision you've been carrying around. Make two lists. On the first, write everything you'd do tomorrow if this decision were already made. On the second, write everything you're currently doing that assumes the decision isn't made yet — every backup plan, every "just in case," every conversation you've been postponing.

The gap between those two lists is the cost of your indecision. It's almost always larger than you expected.

Why We Avoid Committing

The honest reason most decisions don't stick is that committing means accepting a loss. Every choice closes some doors. The version of you who picked the other thing — who took the other job, moved to the other city, said yes instead of no — has to be quietly mourned. People dislike grief, even small grief, so they stall.

But the stall is the worst of both worlds. You don't get the benefit of the path you didn't choose, and you don't get the momentum of the path you did. You get the hallway in between, which is almost always the most uncomfortable place to stand.

A Different Definition of a Good Decision

We tend to grade decisions by their outcomes, but outcomes are corrupted by luck. A more honest definition: a good decision is one you'd be willing to make again, with the same information, even knowing how it turned out.

By that definition, a decision that sticks is already half-good. You gave it the conditions it needed to actually work. Whatever happens next is the world's contribution, not your failure of nerve.

  • Decide once, and out loud. The act of saying it changes the decision's gravity.
  • Burn the bridge in some small way. A canceled meeting, a deleted draft, a returned key.
  • Set a re-evaluation date — and only one. Not "I'll keep an eye on it." A specific day, then trust the decision until then.

Most of the decisions you'll make this year are neither brilliant nor catastrophic. They're ordinary forks in an ordinary road. The art isn't in picking the perfect one. It's in committing fully enough that the road has a chance to take you somewhere.
