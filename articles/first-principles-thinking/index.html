Most people, most of the time, reason by analogy. They look at how things were done before, find a close match to their current situation, and copy the pattern with minor adjustments. It works — until it doesn't. Analogical reasoning quietly anchors you to assumptions that may not even apply, and the result is a slightly improved version of whatever already exists.

First principles thinking takes the opposite approach. Instead of starting from the answers other people arrived at, you strip the problem down to the things you actually know are true, then build back up from there. It's slower. It's harder. And occasionally it produces solutions that nobody else can see.

What First Principles Actually Means

The phrase gets thrown around a lot, often as a synonym for "thinking hard." That's not quite right. A first principle is a foundational assumption — something that can't be deduced from anything more basic. In physics, it's a law. In business, it might be a unit cost or a customer's actual need. The work isn't in coming up with new principles. The work is in refusing to take secondary truths for granted until you've traced them back to something foundational.

If you can't explain why something is true without referring to what someone else told you, you don't actually understand it yet.

A Simple Worked Example

Suppose you're told that batteries are expensive. Reasoning by analogy, you'd accept the premise: batteries cost what batteries cost, and your business plan has to absorb that. End of thought.

Reasoning from first principles, you'd ask: what is a battery actually made of? Cobalt, nickel, aluminum, carbon, some polymers, a steel can. What do those raw materials cost on the commodity market? Add it up. Now compare that number to the retail price of the battery. The gap between those two numbers is the opportunity. The "expensive" wasn't a law of nature — it was a market structure.

This is the move: refuse to accept aggregated truths until you've taken them apart.

Why It's So Uncomfortable

First principles thinking is socially awkward. It requires you to ask questions that sound naive, to admit you don't know things everyone else seems to take for granted, and to spend time on problems that have already been "solved." Most people would rather be confidently wrong with the group than tentatively right alone.

The shortcut that isn't

Analogical reasoning feels like a shortcut, but it has a hidden tax: every time the world changes, your analogies quietly become wrong, and you don't notice until your decisions stop working. First principles takes longer up front, but the foundation is yours. When the world shifts, you can re-derive the answer instead of inheriting an outdated one.

How to Practice It

You don't have to apply first principles to every problem — that would be exhausting and probably counterproductive. Save it for the decisions that matter most: the ones where being wrong is expensive, where conventional wisdom feels suspiciously convenient, or where you suspect the consensus answer is leaving value on the table.

  • Write down what you "know." Then mark each item as derived, told, or fundamental.
  • Attack the "told" items. Where did this come from? Is it still true? Was it ever?
  • Rebuild from the fundamentals. Don't peek at the conventional answer until you've drafted your own.
  • Compare and learn. If your answer matches the consensus, you've earned the confidence to act. If it doesn't, you've found something interesting.

Reasoning by analogy will get you through most days. Reasoning from first principles will get you through the days that actually matter.
