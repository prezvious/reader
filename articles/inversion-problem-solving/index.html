There's a thinking trick that mathematicians, investors, and a small handful of unusually clear-headed people use, and almost nobody else does. It's called inversion. The idea is embarrassingly simple: instead of asking how to achieve what you want, ask how to guarantee its opposite. Then refuse to do those things.

Carl Jacobi, the 19th-century mathematician, summed it up in three words: invert, always invert. He'd reach hard problems by working backward from the failure case. Charlie Munger built half a career on the same move. And once you start using it, you wonder why it took so long to find.

Why Forward Thinking Fails

When you ask "how do I succeed?" you're staring at an open field. The answer space is huge, vague, and seductive. Every plausible-sounding tactic looks like progress. You build long lists of things you could do, and the list itself becomes the substitute for actually doing anything.

When you ask "how do I guarantee failure?" the answer space collapses. The failure modes are usually short, specific, and unambiguous. You can see them. You can name them. And — this is the part that matters — you can usually avoid them, even when you can't reliably do the positive things that would lead to success.

It's remarkable how much long-term advantage people like us have gotten by trying to be consistently not stupid, instead of trying to be very intelligent.

A Worked Example

Suppose you want to be healthy. Forward thinking gives you the usual sprawl: eat better, sleep more, exercise, manage stress, drink water, take vitamins, meditate, get sunlight. It's all true. It's also so broad that you'll probably do none of it.

Now invert. How would you guarantee being unhealthy? You'd sleep four hours a night, drink heavily, sit motionless for fourteen hours a day, eat nothing but ultra-processed food, and never see daylight. That's a much shorter list. And it's a list you can refuse.

Notice what just happened. You didn't have to design an optimal health regimen. You just had to not do five specific things. The ceiling on your health is lower this way, but the floor is dramatically higher — and the floor is where most people's lives actually live.

Where Inversion Shines

Inversion is most powerful when the upside is uncertain but the downside is well understood. That covers a surprising amount of life:

  • Investing. You can't reliably predict winners, but you can recognize the things that bankrupt people: leverage, concentration, panic, fees.
  • Relationships. Nobody really knows what makes a marriage happy. Plenty of people know what makes one miserable: contempt, stonewalling, secrets.
  • Careers. The path to a great career is murky. The path to a stalled one is familiar: never ship anything, never ask for feedback, never leave a job you hate.
  • Writing. You can't formulate a great essay. You can refuse to bury your point, refuse to pad it, refuse to publish it before reading it out loud.

The Discipline of Avoidance

Inversion sounds passive. It isn't. Refusing to do the wrong things is harder than it looks, because the wrong things are usually easy, comfortable, and socially endorsed. Most people drift into the failure modes by default. To avoid them you have to be alert, deliberate, and willing to look boring while everyone around you takes more interesting risks.

The "stupidity audit"

Once a quarter, try this. Write down the three dumbest things you could plausibly do in the next ninety days — the moves that would obviously make your situation worse. Then notice how many of them you're already partway into. Inversion is mostly a pattern-recognition tool, and the patterns are usually already in motion before you spot them.

Why Nobody Uses It

If inversion is so useful, why is it so rare? Three reasons. It feels negative — and we've been told to "stay positive." It looks unambitious — and we've been told to dream big. And it doesn't generate impressive plans — and we've been told that planning is the work.

None of those things is more important than not failing.

The next time you're stuck on a problem, before you reach for another framework or another book, try the simpler move. Ask how you'd guarantee the worst possible outcome. Write the list. And then, with the cold satisfaction of someone who has just made their life much easier, refuse to do any of it.
